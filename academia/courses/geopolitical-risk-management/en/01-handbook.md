## Introduction

For decades, geopolitical risk occupied a footnote position in investment models: a
qualitative variable cited in the "risks" section of research reports and quietly
ignored when building earnings projections. That practice carries a measurable cost.
Portfolios that dismissed geopolitical risk in 2022 absorbed losses that no historical
volatility model had anticipated; those that had incorporated early-warning signals
found asymmetric opportunities in the same environment.

This handbook accompanies the 28-minute masterclass of course ZRC-ACAD-GRM-01. It is
not a video transcript but a fuller development of the six blocks of the course arc:
theory, analytical frameworks, real-world examples and practical tools. Read it before
the session to prime the context, or afterwards to consolidate and deepen what you have
learned.

## Section 1 · Geopolitical Risk as a Price Factor
*Corresponds to the 0–3 min block of the masterclass*

### 1.1 From noise to price

There is a fundamental operational distinction that every investor must internalise: the
difference between **geopolitical noise** and a **geopolitical signal with the capacity
to move prices**.

Noise is abundant. Tensions between major powers are permanent; confrontational
diplomatic rhetoric is routine; and the media amplify every international friction. If
investors reacted to every headline, they would generate unnecessary portfolio turnover
and transaction costs that would erode returns over time.

A signal, by contrast, has three identifiable characteristics: (1) it affects a real
economic flow — energy, goods, capital, or information; (2) it involves actors with
both the capability and the motivation to execute, not merely to threaten; and (3) it
alters the equilibrium of incentives in a way that economic agents must adapt their
behaviour.

Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 met all three criteria weeks before
hostilities began: intelligence signals about troop concentrations were publicly
available, Russia's track record of using energy as a political instrument was well
documented, and the structural incentives for NATO and the EU to respond with
unprecedented sanctions were analytically clear. Yet market consensus maintained an
implied probability of large-scale invasion below 40 % until 72 hours before it
started.

### 1.2 Empirical evidence of geopolitical market impact

Academic literature and market data confirm that geopolitical events have statistically
significant — and in many cases persistent — effects:

- **Energy:** Every major disruption in the Strait of Hormuz since 1973 has generated
  an average crude oil price spike of 20–40 % over the following three months.
- **Sovereign fixed income:** Spreads on local-currency bonds from countries in conflict
  or under sanctions widen by 300–1,500 basis points in the year preceding the event
  and in the six months following it.
- **Currencies:** Emerging-market currencies with high dependence on a single trading
  partner or energy source typically absorb 15–35 % depreciation in the twelve months
  following a material geopolitical escalation.
- **Supply chains:** The Baltic Dry Index and the Shanghai Containerized Freight Index
  have shown correlations above 0.6 with events in key maritime corridors over the
  past decade.

These effects are neither universal nor automatic — they depend on the depth of the
event, the structural dependence of affected economies and the speed of market
repricing — but they are frequent enough and large enough that ignoring them is an
active decision, not a neutral stance.

> **▸ KEY IDEA — Section 1**  
> Geopolitical risk is not a binary event (peace/war); it is a continuous spectrum of
> tensions that translates into altered economic flows. The investor's task is not to
> predict the event but to identify which flows are at risk and what price the market
> is currently assigning to that risk.

## Section 2 · The ZRC Framework: Four Layers of Risk
*Corresponds to the 3–9 min block of the masterclass*

### 2.1 Why a layered framework

A common error in geopolitical analysis applied to investment is working with a single,
diffuse category — "country risk" — that blends phenomena with entirely different time
horizons, transmission mechanisms and reversibility profiles. The ZRC Framework
disaggregates that block into four layers with their own internal logic, enabling the
assignment of distinct monitoring horizons and differentiated response strategies.

### 2.2 The four layers

**Layer 1 — Structural / Macro-political**  
This is the long-run substrate: the quality of state institutions, social cohesion, the
country's geopolitical positioning in the international system and the solidity of the
rule of law. Changes in this layer are slow — measured in decades — but they determine
the ceiling of risk-adjusted returns that an asset can offer. A country with weak
institutions may generate extraordinary short-term returns, but its permanent valuation
ceiling is constrained by the structural risk premium that international markets assign
to it.

*Key variables:* Rule of Law Index (World Justice Project), Fragile States Index (Fund
for Peace), position in military and commercial alliances, historical record on
contracts and private property.

**Layer 2 — Regulatory**  
This layer operates over the medium term (months to years) and captures all normative
changes driven by political decisions: international sanctions, capital controls,
foreign investment restrictions, confiscatory tax changes, local content requirements
or nationalisations. This layer has acquired critical relevance since 2022, when
Western democracies demonstrated both the capability and the willingness to weaponise
the international financial system — SWIFT access, central bank reserves, dollar
settlement — as a geopolitical pressure tool.

*Key variables:* OFAC/SDN lists, EU Council restrictions, FDI screening regulations,
political risk ratings from Oxford Analytica or Control Risks.

**Layer 3 — Operational**  
This is the most immediate layer in terms of impact on corporate results: supply chain
disruption, severed access to critical infrastructure (ports, pipelines, undersea data
cables), interruption of transport routes or loss of access to critical inputs. Events
at this layer tend to be sudden and highly visible, but they also carry greater
reversibility potential once the triggering geopolitical event is resolved or operators
find alternative routes.

*Key variables:* Exposure to high-risk maritime and land corridors, geographic
concentration of suppliers, dependence on inputs with geographically concentrated
production (advanced semiconductors, lithium, rare earths).

**Layer 4 — Market**  
This layer captures how the preceding three translate into observable signals in
financial markets: widening sovereign or corporate spreads, currency depreciation,
portfolio capital outflows, elevated volatility in exposed sectors and appreciation of
safe-haven assets. This layer is the noisiest in the short term — markets overreact
and then correct — but it also offers the best entry and exit opportunities for
investors who have built a solid analytical framework in the upstream layers.

*Key variables:* Sovereign CDS, currency option premia (risk reversal), corporate bond
spreads of exposed issuers, institutional investor positioning in energy and commodity
futures.

> **▸ KEY IDEA — Section 2**  
> The four layers are not independent: an operational shock (Layer 3) that is not
> underpinned by weaknesses in the structural layer (Layer 1) tends to be transitory.
> The severity and reversibility of geopolitical risk are read by assessing how many
> layers are simultaneously compromised.

## Section 3 · Transmission Channels to Asset Prices
*Corresponds to the 9–16 min block of the masterclass*

### 3.1 From geopolitics to price

Identifying geopolitical risk is the first step; the second — more demanding and more
rewarding — is tracing with precision the mechanism through which that risk reaches the
price of a specific asset. There are four primary transmission channels.

### 3.2 The energy channel

Energy is the most direct and most studied transmission channel. Producer and consumer
states have historically used energy flows as a political pressure tool, generating
first- and second-round effects on asset prices.

The first-round mechanism is straightforward: any disruption in the production or
transport of oil, natural gas or electricity raises the cost of those inputs for
consumers and businesses. The second-round mechanism is more complex and more
frequently overlooked: higher energy costs compress industrial margins, push inflation
upward, force central banks to keep rates higher for longer and reduce consumer
purchasing power, deteriorating aggregate growth.

*Recent example:* Russia's decision to sharply curtail gas supplies to Europe in 2022
did not merely send the TTF (European natural gas) price to all-time highs (peak
+1,000 %); it precipitated an industrial crisis in energy-intensive sectors — chemicals,
fertilisers, aluminium, glass — and forced Germany, the largest recipient, to revise
its growth projections down by 1.5–2.5 GDP percentage points for 2023.

### 3.3 The supply chain channel

The second channel operates through the disruption of physical flows of intermediate
and finished goods. Three decades of globalisation have created ultra-efficient but
deeply fragile supply chains: many industrial sectors depend on components manufactured
in a small number of geographies, creating vulnerabilities that only become visible
when geopolitical tension activates them.

Semiconductors are the paradigmatic example: TSMC manufactures in Taiwan between 60 %
and 92 % of global advanced chip production, depending on the technology node. Any
disruption — not necessarily an invasion; a naval or air blockade would be sufficient
— would halt automotive, consumer electronics, medical equipment and defence
production worldwide.

*Recent example:* Houthi attacks in the Red Sea (November 2023–present) forced the
major shipping lines to reroute around the Cape of Good Hope, adding 10–14 transit
days and between USD 1.5 million and USD 3 million in additional costs per voyage for
large container vessels. The Shanghai Containerized Freight Index (SCFI) tripled
between December 2023 and February 2024. The most affected sectors — fast fashion,
electronics, automotive components — saw operating margin compression of 100–300 basis
points in the first half of 2024.

### 3.4 The currency and capital flows channel

The third channel acts on exchange rates and international capital flows. In periods of
elevated geopolitical uncertainty, capital tends to migrate from higher-risk assets
toward safe havens: US dollars, Swiss francs, Treasuries and gold. This movement has
direct effects on emerging-market countries with high dependence on external financing
or on commodities denominated in dollars.

Additionally, financial sanctions can abruptly sever a country's or specific companies'
access to the international payments system — effectively a freeze on their global
financial assets. The most dramatic precedent was the 2022 immobilisation of
USD 300 billion in Russian central bank reserves, an action without modern precedent
that permanently recalibrated the risk calculus of every central bank holding large
reserves in Western currencies.

### 3.5 The risk premium channel

The fourth channel is the most abstract but most pervasive: geopolitics affects the
discount rate investors apply to future cash flows from any asset with exposure to a
territory or sector under tension. This geopolitical risk premium is not always
explicitly labelled in models — it typically appears as a "valuation discount" or a
"multiple haircut" — but it is real and measurable by comparing valuations of similar
assets with different geopolitical profiles.

*Example:* In 2023, European energy infrastructure companies with exposure to Eastern
Europe traded at valuation discounts of 15–25 % relative to their Western European
peers — a discount that could not be explained by operational fundamentals but
reflected the geopolitical risk premium the market had embedded.

> **▸ KEY IDEA — Section 3**  
> The four channels rarely operate in isolation: a single geopolitical event can
> activate all of them simultaneously, generating amplification effects that
> one-dimensional analysis fails to capture. The analytical task is to identify
> the dominant channel for a specific asset and to size its potential magnitude.

## Section 4 · The ZRC Geopolitical Scoring Methodology
*Corresponds to the 16–22 min block of the masterclass*

### 4.1 From qualitative analysis to actionable input

The primary obstacle to integrating geopolitical risk into the investment process is
not a lack of information — today there is nearly unlimited access — but the difficulty
of converting that information into a quantitative input that feeds decision models.
The ZRC Geopolitical Scoring Methodology solves this through a four-step process.

### 4.2 The four steps of ZRC scoring

**Step 1 — Exposure inventory**  
The first step is to map all geopolitical exposures of an asset or portfolio: geographies
where it operates, countries its supply chain depends on, markets where it sells,
currencies in which it reports, and any foreign regulation that affects its activity.
This inventory is not a one-time exercise: it must be updated whenever the asset
changes its operating model or when the geopolitical environment of any relevant
geography changes materially.

**Step 2 — Layer-by-layer evaluation**  
For each relevant geography identified in Step 1, the four ZRC Framework layers are
scored on a scale of 1 (minimal risk) to 5 (severe risk). The assessment draws on
primary and secondary sources: multilateral agency reports (IMF, World Bank, OECD),
political risk analysis platforms (Oxford Analytica, Eurasia Group, Control Risks) and
ZRC Observatory and ZRC Intelligence for near-real-time short-term signals.

**Step 3 — Weighting by transmission channel**  
Not all layers carry equal weight for every asset. A manufacturing company with
production concentrated in a single geography should weight Layer 3 (operational) more
heavily; a financial entity with sovereign debt exposure should weight Layer 2
(regulatory) and Layer 4 (market) more heavily. Weights are defined based on the
business model of the asset being analysed and documented explicitly so they can be
reviewed over time.

**Step 4 — Composite score and decision thresholds**  
The composite score is obtained by multiplying each layer score by its weight and
summing across layers for each geography, then aggregating geographies by their weight
in the portfolio or in the asset's revenue base. The final score falls between 1 and 5
and is interpreted according to three thresholds:

| Score | Level | Recommended action |
|-------|-------|--------------------|
| 1.0–2.0 | Low risk | Routine monitoring (quarterly review) |
| 2.1–3.5 | Moderate risk | Enhanced monitoring + scenario analysis |
| 3.6–5.0 | Elevated risk | Deep geopolitical due diligence + active hedging |

### 4.3 Limits of the scoring approach

Geopolitical scoring is an analysis-structuring tool, not an oracle. Its primary
limitations are: (1) it depends on the quality and currency of its input data; (2) it
does not capture genuine "black swan" events well; (3) it can create false precision
if analysts forget that weights and scores are expert judgements, not objective facts.
Using it correctly means revisiting it frequently and combining it with qualitative
scenario analysis.

> **▸ KEY IDEA — Section 4**  
> A geopolitical score does not replace analytical judgement — it structures it. Its
> value lies in forcing explicitness: compelling the investor to name the exposures,
> the layers at risk and the transmission channels before deciding. What is named can
> be managed; what remains implicit cannot.

## Section 5 · Case Study — Exposure to a Critical Logistics Corridor
*Corresponds to the 22–26 min block of the masterclass*

### 5.1 Case overview

**Asset analysed:** A mid-size European industrial company (revenue ~€800 M) producing
precision machinery with an electronic components supply chain concentrated in East
Asia. Sixty-five percent of its critical components transit through the Red Sea and
the Suez Canal.

**Geopolitical trigger:** Escalation of Houthi attacks in the Red Sea from November
2023 onward, followed by the decision of major shipping lines to reroute vessels via
the Cape of Good Hope.

### 5.2 Applying the ZRC Framework

**Layer 1 — Structural:** Yemen has been in active civil war since 2014. Yemeni state
fragility is structural with no near- or medium-term resolution in sight. This
information was not new in 2023 — it had not had direct operational impact on the
company as long as Red Sea maritime traffic remained uninterrupted.

**Layer 2 — Regulatory:** Houthi operations did not initially generate sanctions
directly applicable to the company, but they did activate the European naval operation
Aspides, which modified the maritime insurance context and the liability conditions of
marine underwriters in the area.

**Layer 3 — Operational:** The impact was immediate and material. Transit times from
Asia to Europe extended by 10–14 days; freight rates tripled; and hull and cargo
insurance in the zone multiplied 5–10 times. For the company, this translated into
higher working capital requirements (more inventory in transit), margin pressure on
fixed-price contracts signed before the event, and delivery delays that triggered
penalty clauses.

**Layer 4 — Market:** Shares of companies with high Asia-Europe logistics dependence
corrected an average of 8–15 % in January–February 2024. The European precision
machinery sub-sector traded at a P/E discount of 1.5–2.0 turns below its historical
average during the first quarter, with analysts specifically citing supply chain risk
in their downgrades.

### 5.3 Scoring and decision

| Layer | Score (1–5) | Weight for this asset |
|-------|-------------|----------------------|
| Structural | 4 | 20 % |
| Regulatory | 2 | 15 % |
| Operational | 5 | 45 % |
| Market | 3 | 20 % |
| **Composite score** | **3.85** | — |

The score of 3.85 placed the company in the "Elevated risk" band, which would have
triggered deep geopolitical due diligence and evaluation of active hedges: analysis of
the company's capacity to absorb higher logistics costs, assessment of flexibility to
diversify suppliers outside the Asia-Suez corridor, and a defensive positioning in
the equity or in sector derivatives.

> **▸ KEY IDEA — Section 5**  
> Geopolitical analysis of an asset does not begin when the crisis erupts — it begins
> at initial due diligence. The structural vulnerability of the Red Sea corridor was
> analytically visible years before the 2023 escalation. Investors who incorporated
> it into their operational risk analysis were positioned to act rather than react.

## Section 6 · Synthesis and Next Steps
*Corresponds to the 26–28 min block of the masterclass*

### 6.1 Three ideas to take away

This course has developed an argument across three levels:

1. **Geopolitical risk already has a price** — not incorporating it is taking an
   implicit position that it is worth zero, which is almost always wrong.
2. **The ZRC Four-Layer Framework enables disaggregation** — converting what would
   otherwise be a vague, non-actionable category into measurable variables with
   distinct horizons and mechanisms.
3. **The score is the beginning, not the end** — its value lies in structuring the
   analysis and forcing the explicitness of assumptions, not in producing a number
   that replaces judgement.

## Glossary

**Capital controls**  
Government-imposed restrictions on the flow of capital into or out of a country,
typically deployed during balance-of-payments crises or geopolitical tensions. They
can materially impair an investor's ability to repatriate returns or exit a position.

**Composite geopolitical score (ZRC)**  
A numerical indicator (scale 1–5) that aggregates the assessment of the four ZRC
Framework layers, weighted by their relevance to a specific asset or portfolio.
Determines the monitoring level and the type of response recommended.

**Country risk premium**  
The additional yield demanded by investors to hold assets from a specific country,
above the return of a comparable asset without that country exposure. Reflects the
market's aggregated view of political, regulatory, operational and macroeconomic risks.

**Critical logistics corridor**  
A transport route — maritime, land or air — whose disruption carries systemic
consequences for international trade. Key examples: Strait of Hormuz (oil), Suez Canal
and Red Sea (Asia-Europe trade), Strait of Malacca (Asian LNG), Taiwan Strait
(advanced semiconductors).

**Financial weaponisation**  
The deliberate use of access to the international financial system — SWIFT, reserves
in reserve currencies, settlement systems — as a geopolitical pressure tool. The 2022
immobilisation of Russian central bank reserves is the defining modern precedent.

**Geopolitical due diligence**  
A systematic process of evaluating political and security risk applied to an
investment, analogous in logic to financial due diligence but focused on non-financial
variables: institutions, international relations, social stability and regulatory
frameworks.

**Geopolitical signal**  
A data point or event that, when analysed through an appropriate framework, anticipates
a material change in the political or security environment with the capacity to affect
asset prices. Distinguished from noise by its linkage to real economic flows and to
actors with execution capability.

**Geopolitical tail risk**  
A low-probability, high-impact scenario at the negative tail of an asset's return
distribution, caused or exacerbated by geopolitical factors.

**Geographic concentration risk**  
Vulnerability arising from having a high proportion of inputs, production or markets
concentrated in a single geography or corridor, which amplifies the impact of
localised geopolitical events.

**Nearshoring / Reshoring**  
The trend to relocate industrial production or parts of the supply chain to the home
country (reshoring) or to geographically and culturally proximate locations
(nearshoring), motivated in part by the perceived increase in geopolitical risk in
global supply chains.

**Operational layer (ZRC Framework)**  
The dimension of geopolitical risk covering disruptions to an asset's physical
operations: supply chains, infrastructure access, logistics and access to critical
inputs. Events at this layer tend to be sudden but often reversible once the
triggering event resolves.

**Risk reversal (FX options)**  
The difference in implied volatility between out-of-the-money call and put options on
a currency pair. Used as a market-based indicator of directional risk perception; a
sharp widening in risk reversal in an emerging-market currency pair can signal
geopolitical stress before it appears in spot rates.

**Secondary sanction**  
A coercive measure that not only prohibits direct transactions with the sanctions
target but also penalises third parties that maintain commercial or financial
relationships with that target. Its extraterritorial reach makes it one of the most
impactful tools of financial geopolitics.

**Sovereign CDS (Credit Default Swap)**  
A derivative instrument that transfers the default risk of a sovereign issuer. Its
premium (spread) is a market indicator of perceived country risk and typically reacts
to geopolitical deterioration before rating agencies update their assessments.

**Structural layer (ZRC Framework)**  
The long-run dimension of geopolitical risk reflecting institutional quality,
social cohesion and a country's positioning in the international system. Changes in
this layer are slow but determine the permanent ceiling of risk-adjusted returns
available from assets exposed to that country.

## Recommended Readings and Resources

### Books

- **Blackwill, R. D. & Harris, J. M.** — *War by Other Means: Geoeconomics and
  Statecraft* (Harvard University Press, 2016). The foundational conceptual framework
  for understanding geoeconomics as an extension of geopolitics.
- **Luttwak, E. N.** — *Strategy: The Logic of War and Peace* (Harvard University
  Press, 2002). The classic reference for understanding the strategic logic of state
  actors.
- **Zeihan, P.** — *The End of the World Is Just the Beginning* (Harper Business,
  2022). Analysis of how the reversal of globalisation is reshaping supply chains and
  investment geographies.
- **Bremmer, I.** — *The End of the Free Market: Who Wins the War Between States and
  Corporations?* (Portfolio, 2010). Essential reading on state capitalism and political
  risk for equity investors.

  armed conflicts. [ucdp.uu.se]

  investors.

  to the generalist investor.
