## Exercise 1 · Mapping the Four Risk Layers

**Reference block:** ZRC Framework — The Four Layers (3–9 min of the masterclass)  
**Estimated time:** 25 minutes

### Brief

Apply the ZRC four-layer framework to the scenario described below. For each
layer, identify the most relevant risk variables, justify their severity level
(Low / Moderate / Elevated / Severe), and determine the typical time horizon
in which that layer operates for this asset.

### Scenario: Turkey as an investment geography (2024–2025)

A pan-European fund manager is evaluating a significant investment in a Turkish
maritime logistics infrastructure operator with concessions in the Sea of Marmara
and the Bosphorus Strait. The available context points are:

**Macro-political and structural context:**
- Turkey is a NATO member but maintains active commercial and military relations
  with Russia (purchase of the S-400 anti-missile system; bilateral trade with
  Russia grew +85% in 2022–2023).
- In 2023–2024 Turkey applied heterodox monetary policies (negative real interest
  rates for months), triggering an inflationary crisis: peak CPI of 85% year-on-year
  in October 2022, with lasting effects through 2024.
- The Turkish lira (TRY) lost approximately 80% of its value against the dollar
  between 2018 and 2024.
- The government has accumulated a track record of judicial interference and
  measures against foreign companies in dispute with Turkish authorities.
- Turkey controls passage through the Bosphorus and Dardanelles under the Montreux
  Convention (1936), granting it the capacity to block military transit in wartime.

**Regulatory context:**
- In 2023, minimum "local participation" requirements (51%) were introduced for
  the renewal of strategic infrastructure concessions.
- The Turkish central bank maintains informal controls on foreign currency purchases
  and overseas transfers above certain thresholds.
- Turkey is not party to bilateral investment treaties (BITs) with several EU
  countries following the termination of existing agreements.

**Operational context:**
- The Bosphorus Strait channels 3–4% of global seaborne oil trade; any closure —
  voluntary or forced — carries systemic impact.
- The target operator's concessions expire in 2031; renewal depends on the Turkish
  Ministry of Transport.
- The operator holds handling contracts with four of the world's top-10 shipping lines.

**Market context:**
- Turkey's sovereign CDS trades at ~300–350 bp (June 2024), significantly above the
  average for investment-grade emerging markets.
- Turkish equity (BIST-100 index) trades at a historical discount in P/BV vs EM
  peers: 0.7× against a 1.4× average.
- Fixed income analysts apply an explicit "Turkish discount" of 150–200 bp
  in infrastructure asset valuation models.

## Exercise 3 · ZRC Mini Geopolitical Score (0–100 scale)

**Reference block:** Scoring methodology (16–22 min of the masterclass)  
**Estimated time:** 35 minutes

### Brief

A European infrastructure fund is conducting due diligence on two alternative port
assets for a minority equity investment (15%). Both assets have similar operational and
financial fundamentals (same EBITDA, same debt, same infrastructure quality). The
decision hinges on differential geopolitical risk.

Build a mini geopolitical score for each asset using the ZRC methodology. Use the
0–100 scale (100 = maximum risk) derived by scoring each layer between 0 and 25,
with the weightings indicated. Support each score with at least one argument per layer.

### Asset A: Port of Ain Sokhna (Gulf of Suez, Egypt)

- Port at the southern entrance of the Suez Canal; strategic for Asia-Europe trade.
- Concessionaire: joint venture between an Egyptian state operator and a European partner.
- Egypt's external debt is at critical levels (~160% of exports in 2024) with high
  dependence on IMF and Gulf financing.
- The IMF approved an $8 billion programme in 2024 with structural adjustment conditions.
- The Egyptian government has nationalised foreign investment assets in at least two
  documented episodes since 2010.
- Security risk: Houthi attacks in the Red Sea in 2023–2024 reduced Suez Canal
  traffic by 42%; the effect on port tariffs is direct.
- The Suez Canal generates ~$10 billion annually in foreign exchange for Egypt;
  the government has strong incentives to protect the infrastructure.

### Asset B: Port of Da Nang (central Vietnam)

- Port in the South China Sea, an area of active territorial disputes between
  China, Vietnam, the Philippines, Malaysia, and Brunei.
- Vietnam has a generally favourable foreign investment framework with a track record
  of contract compliance (no documented nationalisations since 1990).
- Vietnam–China relations are complex: China is Vietnam's top trading partner but
  a live territorial dispute exists, with regular naval incidents.
- The Vietnamese government recently aligned its foreign policy with the U.S.
  (Biden visit, 2023 defence agreement); this reduces the risk of Western sanctions
  but may increase pressure from China.
- Stable local context: single-party state, comparatively low corruption in Southeast
  Asia, predictable regulatory frameworks for infrastructure investment.
- Vietnam sovereign CDS: ~150 bp (below Egypt's ~450 bp).

### Scoring rubric

For each layer, assign a score from 0 to 25 (0 = no risk, 25 = maximum risk).

| Layer | Weight | Asset A: Ain Sokhna | Asset B: Da Nang |
|-------|--------|---------------------|------------------|
| 1 — Structural | × 1.0 | __ / 25 | __ / 25 |
| 2 — Regulatory | × 1.2 | __ / 25 | __ / 25 |
| 3 — Operational | × 1.3 | __ / 25 | __ / 25 |
| 4 — Market | × 0.8 | __ / 25 | __ / 25 |
| **Weighted score (0–100)** | | **__ / 100** | **__ / 100** |

> **Methodological note:** The weighted score is calculated as:  
> (L1 × 1.0 + L2 × 1.2 + L3 × 1.3 + L4 × 0.8) / 4.3 × 100  
> The weightings reflect that for port infrastructure assets,  
> operational and regulatory risk are more determinative than structural risk.

### Expected deliverable

1. The completed table with scores and weighted totals for both assets.
2. One justification argument per layer per asset (max. 2 lines per cell).
3. A 100-word conclusion answering: which asset would you recommend to the fund
   on geopolitical grounds alone, and under what conditions would that recommendation change?

## Exercise 4 · Investment Decision Memo (one page)

**Reference block:** Case study and synthesis (22–28 min of the masterclass)  
**Estimated time:** 35 minutes

### Brief

Write a one-page investment decision memo (max. 500 words) in the format you
would use to present to an investment committee. The memo must integrate the ZRC
layer geopolitical analysis with a clear, actionable recommendation, including
proposed hedges and review triggers.

The memo must follow the structure set out below. Length is not assessed — precision,
information hierarchy, and coherence between the analysis and the recommendation are.

### Scenario: Investment in the Tanger Med II port operator (Morocco)

A Spanish infrastructure fund is evaluating a 12% stake in the operator of
Tanger Med's second container terminal — the largest port in Africa and one of the
principal transhipment hubs of the western Mediterranean.

**Financial data:**
- 2023 Revenue: €420M | EBITDA margin: 52% | Net debt/EBITDA: 3.1×
- Fund target IRR: 11–13% in EUR
- Intended holding period: 7–9 years

**Geopolitical context:**
- Morocco normalised diplomatic relations with Israel in 2020 (Abraham Accords),
  improving relations with the U.S. but generating tensions with some Arab states.
- Morocco–Spain relations are volatile: a serious diplomatic crisis occurred in
  2021–2022 (Brahim Ghali / Western Sahara episode) leading to a temporary border
  closure. Relations normalised in 2023 with new cooperation agreements.
- Morocco holds a strategic position in the Strait of Gibraltar: 20% of global
  maritime trade transits it. This confers negotiating leverage but also
  significant international strategic visibility.
- The Moroccan government has consistently applied the foreign investor guarantee
  framework. No nationalisation episodes in the last 30 years.
- Tanger Med currently processes 9 million TEUs annually; the Tanger Med III
  expansion is in the licensing phase and could add 3 million TEUs by 2027–2028.
- Physical climate risk: moderate-to-high seismic risk zone (proximity to the
  Gibraltar Strait fault line).

**Prior geopolitical score (analysis team):**
- Layer 1 (Structural): 9/25 · Layer 2 (Regulatory): 7/25
- Layer 3 (Operational): 14/25 · Layer 4 (Market): 8/25
- **Composite score: 38/100 → Moderate Risk**

### Exercise 1 — Reference solution

**Layer table — Turkey:**

| Layer | Key variables | Severity | Horizon |
|-------|---------------|----------|---------|
| Structural | Erosion of judicial independence; ambiguous geopolitical pivot (NATO + S-400 + Russia); track record of interference in foreign firms | **Elevated** | Decades |
| Regulatory | 51% local participation requirement for concessions; informal FX controls; absence of BITs with several EU states; forced renegotiation risk (concession expires 2031) | **Elevated** | 1–3 years |
| Operational | Concession non-renewal risk; dependence on ministerial authorisation; Bosphorus position as strategic asset (double-edged: attractive but regulated by Montreux, outside operator control) | **Moderate** | Immediate–short |
| Market | Sovereign CDS 300–350 bp; TRY −80% vs USD over 6 years; explicit analyst discount (+150–200 bp); local equity undervalued by risk premium | **Elevated** | Continuous |

**Reference synthesis paragraph:**  
All four layers are simultaneously compromised, placing total risk in the severe-to-elevated
band. The distinctive feature of the Turkish case is that the layers are not independent:
institutional erosion (L1) directly feeds regulatory risk (L2) and the market discount (L4).
Only the operational layer is moderate, and only because the asset benefits from the
Bosphorus's strategic position. Reversibility is low for layers 1 and 2 (horizons of
years to decades) and higher for layers 3 and 4, though these absorb the most short-term
volatility. The number of compromised layers suggests the risk is not transitory and
that any investment would require very robust structural hedges.

|---------|-----------|--------|-------|
| Supply chain | TSMC chip production disruption → production line stoppages | **Severely negative** | Immediate (days 4–10) |
| Risk premia | Re-rating of tech/auto sector for Taiwan exposure → multiple compression | **Negative** | Immediate |
| Currencies & capital | Capital outflow from European assets with China exposure; USD appreciation | **Negative** | Short-term |

_Recommendation:_ Reduce exposure tactically (stop-loss at −10%). Evaluate puts on European auto sector. Review on day 7 based on TSMC production status.

**Position B (container shipping HY bonds):**

| Channel | Mechanism | Impact | Speed |
|---------|-----------|--------|-------|
| Supply chain | Trans-Pacific route diversions increase distances and operating costs | **Ambiguous** (rates rise, costs rise too) | Immediate |
| Risk premia | HY spread widening from generalised risk-off; higher operational risk premium | **Negative** | Immediate |
| Capital flows | Flight to quality compresses HY demand; potential redemptions from HY funds | **Negative** | Short-term |

_Recommendation:_ Hedge via issuer CDS or reduce position by 50%. Rising freight rates may partially offset spread widening but not at bond level.

**Position C (materials/mining fund):**

| Channel | Mechanism | Impact | Speed |
|---------|-----------|--------|-------|
| Supply chain | China's threatened rare earth export restriction → price spike benefits producers | **Positive (rare earths)** | Short-term |
| Sanctions/regulatory | U.S.–China tech sanctions may affect Chinese producer operations | **Negative (40% allocation)** | Medium-term |
| Risk premia | 25% in LatAm copper is relative safe haven vs escalation scenario | **Relatively positive** | Immediate |

_Recommendation:_ Hold with short-term positive bias (rare earths). Monitor Chinese producer exposure: if the scenario intensifies, the China allocation may become sanction-affected.

### Exercise 4 — Reference solution

_(Reference memo for the instructor — students may reach the same conclusion by different analytical paths)_

**Recommendation:** INVEST WITH CONDITIONS

**Core logic:** A score of 38/100 places the asset in moderate risk, compatible with
the target IRR of 11–13% provided the hedges below are incorporated. The operational
layer (14/25) is dominant and reflects the risk of traffic disruption in the Strait
of Gibraltar and the volatility of Morocco–Spain relations. The regulatory layer (7/25)
is the most favourable, underpinned by 30 years without expropriations and Morocco's
strong incentive to preserve Tanger Med's attractiveness to investors.

**Reference hedges and conditions:**
- Regulatory stabilisation clause in the SPA with ICSID arbitration seat.
- Political risk insurance (MIGA or CESCE equivalent) covering direct expropriation
  and dividend repatriation difficulty.
- Score review every 6 months with automatic trigger for additional due diligence
  if the score exceeds 55/100.

**Review triggers:**
1. Deterioration of Morocco–Spain relations affecting the 2023 cooperation agreements.
2. Security incident in the Strait of Gibraltar with impact on maritime traffic.
3. Commencement of Morocco–China negotiations on Tanger Med III concessions.
4. Change of government in Morocco with a platform to revise concessions held by foreign investors.
