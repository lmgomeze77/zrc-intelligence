## [00:00 – 03:00] BLOCK 1 · Geopolitical Risk as a Price Factor

[SLIDE 1 — Title card: "Geopolitical Risk Management for Investors · Zenith Academia"]

On the 24th of February, 2022, at five in the morning Moscow time, Russia launched its
full-scale invasion of Ukraine. In the seventy-two hours that followed, the MOEX
index collapsed forty-five percent. The ruble lost thirty percent of its value in days.
And European natural gas prices — the TTF benchmark — began a climb that would take
them more than ten times higher before the year was out.

[PAUSE]

How many of the fund managers and investors who had exposure to those assets had
incorporated that risk into their models?

Fewer than forty percent of the market assigned a meaningful probability to a
large-scale invasion — even with troop concentration signals that had been available in
public sources since November the year before.

That gap — between the signal that was there and the price that ignored it — is exactly
the problem this course is designed to solve.

[SLIDE 2 — Statement: "Geopolitical risk already has a price. Ignoring it means implicitly pricing it at zero."]

Welcome to Zenith Academia. I'm an analyst at Zenith Rise Capital, and over the next
twenty-eight minutes we're going to build a practical framework together — for
identifying, measuring and integrating geopolitical risk into investment decisions.

We are not doing geopolitics for its own intellectual pleasure. We're doing it because
it has a price. Because there is a spread widening, a currency depreciation, a margin
collapse at the other end of every material geopolitical event. And the difference
between the investor who anticipates it and the one who reacts can be measured in
percentage points of return.

[SLIDE 3 — Data: measurable geopolitical impact — energy, sovereign fixed income, currencies]

Let me give you three data points to move this from intuition to evidence.

First: every major disruption in the Strait of Hormuz since 1973 has generated an
average crude oil price spike of twenty to forty percent over the following three
months.

Second: spreads on local-currency bonds from countries under conflict or sanctions
widen by three hundred to fifteen hundred basis points in the year before the event.
The year before. The signal was in the market before anyone framed it as news.

Third: emerging-market currencies with high dependence on a single trading partner or
energy source absorb fifteen to thirty-five percent depreciation in the twelve months
following a material geopolitical escalation.

[PAUSE]

Geopolitical risk already has a price. What most investors are missing is the framework
to read it.

## [03:00 – 09:00] BLOCK 2 · The ZRC Framework: Four Layers of Risk

[SLIDE 4 — Title: "The ZRC Framework · Four Layers of Geopolitical Risk"]

The most common mistake I see in geopolitical analysis applied to investment is not a
lack of information. Today we have more geopolitical information available than at any
point in history. The mistake is collapsing everything into one blurry category —
"country risk", "political risk" — that blends phenomena with entirely different time
horizons, different transmission mechanisms and, critically, different reversibility
profiles.

A country can have fragile institutions going back decades — that is structural risk.
And at the same time be experiencing a balance-of-payments crisis that is triggering
capital controls right now — that is regulatory risk. And simultaneously have a border
conflict cutting a specific supply chain — that is operational risk. And all of that
together be moving through the spreads on its sovereign bonds — that is market risk.

Four distinct phenomena. If we analyse them as one, we will never know which is
dominant, which is transitory and which one actually warrants action.

At ZRC, we call this the Four-Layer Framework.

[SLIDE 5 — Diagram: "Layer 1 — Structural / Macro-political"]

The first layer is the structural layer. This is the long-run substrate: the quality
of state institutions, social cohesion, the country's geopolitical positioning in the
international system and the solidity of the rule of law.

Changes at this layer are slow. They are measured in decades. But they determine
something critical: the ceiling of risk-adjusted returns that an asset in that country
can sustainably offer you.

Think through the practical implication. A country with weak institutions can deliver
extraordinary short-term returns. Carry trades in fragile currencies can be spectacular
while they last. But the permanent valuation ceiling of any asset in that country is
constrained by the structural risk premium the market assigns to it. And that premium
does not disappear on the back of one good macroeconomic quarter.

The variables we monitor here are the Rule of Law Index from the World Justice Project,
the Fragile States Index from the Fund for Peace, the country's positioning in military
and commercial alliances and its historical track record on contracts and private
property.

[SLIDE 6 — Diagram: "Layer 2 — Regulatory" and "Layer 3 — Operational"]

The second layer is the regulatory layer. It operates over the medium term — months to
years — and captures all normative changes driven by political decisions: international
sanctions, capital controls, foreign investment restrictions, confiscatory tax changes
or nationalisations.

This layer has gained critical relevance since 2022, and I want to be specific about
why. Because in that year, Western democracies demonstrated both the capability and
the willingness to weaponise the international financial system — SWIFT access, central
bank reserves, dollar settlement — as a geopolitical pressure tool.

The immobilisation of three hundred billion dollars in Russian central bank reserves
was an act without modern precedent. And that precedent permanently recalibrated the
risk calculus of every institutional investor with exposure to countries whose
geopolitical positioning is not aligned with the West. The question "can my assets be
frozen by regulatory action?" is no longer theoretical.

The third layer is the operational layer. This is the most immediate in terms of
corporate earnings impact: supply chain disruption, severed access to critical
infrastructure — ports, pipelines, undersea data cables — interruption of transport
routes or loss of access to critical inputs.

Events at this layer tend to be sudden and high-visibility. They make every front page.
But they also — in many cases, not all — carry greater reversibility once the
triggering event resolves or operators find alternative routes.

[SLIDE 7 — Diagram: "Layer 4 — Market" + four-layer summary table]

The fourth layer is the market layer. It captures how the first three translate into
observable financial market signals: widening sovereign or corporate spreads, currency
depreciation, portfolio capital outflows, elevated volatility in exposed sectors and
appreciation of safe-haven assets.

This layer is the noisiest in the short term. Markets overreact. Then correct. Then
overreact again. But — and this is the key point — it is also the layer that offers
the best entry and exit opportunities for the investor who has built a solid analytical
foundation in the upstream layers.

Let me leave you with one idea before we move on. The four layers are not independent.
An operational shock that is not underpinned by structural weakness tends to be
transitory. The severity and reversibility of geopolitical risk are read by assessing
how many layers are simultaneously compromised. The more layers in play at once, the
more persistent the impact.

## [09:00 – 16:00] BLOCK 3 · Transmission Channels to Asset Prices

[SLIDE 8 — Title: "Four channels. One price."]

Identifying the geopolitical risk is the first step. The second — more demanding and
more rewarding — is tracing with precision the mechanism by which that risk reaches the
price of a specific asset.

Because there is an enormous difference between knowing that "there is geopolitical
tension in the Red Sea" and knowing that "that tension is going to compress the
operating margins of European industrial companies with Asia-Europe supply chains by
one hundred to three hundred basis points in the first quarter of 2024". The first is
an observation. The second is an investment input.

There are four primary transmission channels.

[SLIDE 9 — Channel 1: "Energy" with TTF and Hormuz data]

The first is the energy channel. It is the most direct and the most studied. Producer
and consumer states have historically used energy flows as a political pressure tool.

The first-round mechanism is straightforward: any disruption in the production or
transport of oil, natural gas or electricity raises the cost of those inputs for
consumers and businesses.

But the second-round mechanism is what most analysts underestimate. Higher energy costs
compress industrial margins. That pushes inflation upward. That forces central banks
to keep rates higher for longer. And that reduces consumer purchasing power,
deteriorating aggregate growth.

This is not an abstract chain. The most recent and clearest example: Russia's sharp
curtailment of gas supplies to Europe in 2022 did not just send the TTF — European
natural gas — up more than a thousand percent from its lows. It precipitated an
industrial crisis in the most energy-intensive sectors: chemicals, fertilisers,
aluminium, glass. Germany, the largest recipient, revised its growth projections down
between one-and-a-half and two-and-a-half GDP percentage points for 2023.

That is not noise. That is a price factor with a name.

[SLIDE 10 — Channel 2: "Supply chains" with the semiconductor-Taiwan case]

The second channel operates through the disruption of physical flows of intermediate
and finished goods.

Three decades of globalisation have created ultra-efficient but deeply fragile supply
chains. Many industrial sectors depend on components manufactured in a small number of
geographies, creating vulnerabilities that only become visible when geopolitical
tension activates them.

I want you to think for a moment about semiconductors. TSMC, the Taiwanese company,
manufactures between sixty and ninety-two percent of the world's advanced chip
production, depending on the technology node. Not sixty percent of one type of chip.
Sixty to ninety-two percent of every sub-five-nanometre chip the world uses.

What would happen if there were a disruption in Taiwan? Not necessarily an invasion.
A partial naval blockade would be enough. A governance crisis that halted production
for a few weeks. The impact would propagate immediately to automotive, consumer
electronics, medical equipment and defence production worldwide. And financial markets
would price it in before the physical supply chain felt it.

That risk exists today. The tension signals in the Taiwan Strait — air force
incursions, increasingly large-scale naval exercises, shifts in the Communist Party's
official rhetoric — are observable in real time. The question is whether your analysis
of companies with advanced semiconductor exposure includes that vulnerability or
ignores it.

[SLIDE 11 — Channel 3: "Currencies and capital flows" with the Russian reserves case]

The third channel acts on exchange rates and international capital flows.

In periods of elevated geopolitical uncertainty, capital migrates from higher-risk
assets toward safe havens: US dollars, Swiss francs, Treasuries, gold. That movement
has direct effects on emerging-market countries with high dependence on external
financing or on commodities denominated in dollars.

But the most important story in this channel over the last few years is not private
capital movement. It is the weaponisation of the financial system.

When the West immobilised three hundred billion dollars in Russian central bank
reserves in 2022, it was not just a sanction. It was a message to every central bank
in the world: if your geopolitical positioning conflicts with Western interests, your
reserves in dollars, euros and sterling are not safe.

[PAUSE]

The consequences of that precedent are still unfolding. The acceleration in reserve
diversification toward gold, renminbi and alternative assets that we have observed
since 2022 in emerging-market central banks is not coincidence. It is a rational
response to a risk that was previously considered theoretical.

[SLIDE 12 — Channel 4: "Risk premia" with valuation discount example]

The fourth channel is the most abstract but perhaps the most pervasive: geopolitics
affects the discount rate investors apply to future cash flows from any asset with
exposure to a territory or sector under tension.

This geopolitical risk premium does not always appear explicitly labelled in models.
It typically surfaces as a "valuation discount" or a "multiple haircut". But it is
real and it is measurable — by comparing valuations of similar assets with different
geopolitical profiles.

In 2023, European energy infrastructure companies with exposure to Eastern Europe
traded at valuation discounts of fifteen to twenty-five percent relative to their
Western European peers. That discount could not be explained by operational
fundamentals. It reflected the geopolitical risk premium the market had embedded.

And that creates two types of situation: assets that deserve the discount because the
risk is real and persistent, and assets that are undervalued because the market has
applied an excessive or transitory risk premium. The analytical framework is what
allows you to tell them apart.

## [16:00 – 22:00] BLOCK 4 · The ZRC Geopolitical Scoring Methodology

[SLIDE 13 — Title: "From signal to decision · ZRC Scoring"]

We have covered what geopolitical risk is, how it breaks down into layers and how it
reaches asset prices. Now comes the question I get most often when we present this
approach to investors: "This all sounds compelling, but how do I turn it into a
number? How does this enter my model?"

It is exactly the right question. And it has an answer.

The primary obstacle to integrating geopolitical risk into the investment process is
not a lack of information — it is the difficulty of converting that information, which
is largely qualitative, into a quantitative input that can enter decision models.
The ZRC Scoring Methodology solves that problem in four steps.

[SLIDE 14 — Steps 1 and 2 of the scoring process]

Step one is the exposure inventory. Before you evaluate anything, you need to map all
the geopolitical exposures of an asset or portfolio: the geographies where it operates,
the countries its supply chain depends on, the markets where it sells, the currencies
in which it reports, and any foreign regulation that affects its activity.

This inventory is not a one-time exercise. It needs updating whenever the asset changes
its operating model — an acquisition, a new product line, a new market — and whenever
the geopolitical environment of any relevant geography changes materially.

Step two is the layer-by-layer evaluation. For each geography identified in step one,
you assess the four ZRC Framework layers on a scale of one to five: one is minimal
risk, five is severe risk.

This assessment is not unconstrained opinion. It draws on primary and secondary
sources: reports from the IMF, World Bank and OECD; political risk analysis platforms
such as Oxford Analytica, Eurasia Group or Control Risks; and the near-real-time
short-term signals captured by ZRC Observatory and ZRC Intelligence.

[SLIDE 15 — Steps 3 and 4: weighting and decision threshold table]

Step three is weighting by transmission channel. Not all layers carry equal weight for
every asset. A manufacturing company with production concentrated in a single geography
should weight the operational layer more heavily. A financial entity with sovereign
debt exposure should weight regulatory and market layers more heavily.

Weights are defined based on the business model of the asset being analysed — and,
importantly, they are documented explicitly. Not as a bureaucratic exercise, but
because documenting the weights allows them to be revisited when circumstances change,
and because it surfaces the implicit assumptions that would otherwise remain hidden
in the analysis.

Step four is the composite score and decision thresholds. Multiply each layer score by
its weight, sum across layers, and you have a number between one and five. That number
determines the response level.

A score between one and two signals low risk: routine quarterly monitoring. Between
two-point-one and three-point-five, moderate risk: enhanced monitoring plus scenario
analysis. Above three-point-six, elevated risk: deep geopolitical due diligence and
evaluation of active hedges.

[SLIDE 16 — Scoring limits and note on analytical judgement]

Before we move to the case study, I want to be direct about what this scoring cannot do.

It does not capture genuine black-swan events well — the ones that nobody in the market
is pricing because by definition they are not appearing in the information sources
feeding the model. It does not eliminate analyst bias: if the person running the
scoring systematically overestimates the stability of a particular type of regime, the
model will reproduce that bias. And it can create false precision if analysts forget
that the scores are expert judgements, not objective facts.

Using it well means revisiting it frequently, combining it with qualitative scenario
analysis, and above all remembering that its value lies in structuring the analysis and
forcing explicit assumptions — not in producing a number that replaces human judgement.

## [22:00 – 26:00] BLOCK 5 · Case Study

[SLIDE 17 — Case title card: "ZRC Case Study · Exposure to a Critical Logistics Corridor"]

Let us now apply everything we have covered to a real scenario.

The asset is a mid-size European industrial company — revenue around eight hundred
million euros — manufacturing precision machinery. Its electronic component supply
chain is concentrated in East Asia. Sixty-five percent of its critical components
transit through the Red Sea and the Suez Canal.

The geopolitical trigger: the escalation of Houthi attacks in the Red Sea from November
2023 onward, followed by the decision of the major shipping lines — Maersk, Hapag-Lloyd,
CMA-CGM — to reroute their vessels around the Cape of Good Hope.

[SLIDE 18 — Layer assessment: Yemen, Red Sea, Asia-Europe corridor]

We apply the ZRC Framework.

Structural layer: Yemen has been in active civil war since 2014. Yemeni state fragility
is structural — not a cyclical crisis, and with no near- or medium-term resolution in
prospect. Score: four out of five. This was not new information in November 2023. It
had been observable for nearly a decade.

Regulatory layer: the Houthi operations did not initially generate sanctions directly
applicable to the company, but they did activate the European naval operation Aspides —
which modified the maritime insurance context and the liability conditions of marine
underwriters in the area. Score: two out of five. Moderate impact, not critical for
this specific company.

Operational layer: this is where the impact was immediate and material. Transit times
from Asia to Europe extended by ten to fourteen days. Freight rates tripled. Hull and
cargo insurance in the zone multiplied five to ten times. For the company, that meant
higher working capital requirements — more inventory in transit — margin pressure on
fixed-price contracts signed before the event, and delivery delays that triggered
contractual penalty clauses. Score: five out of five. Severe impact.

Market layer: shares of companies with high Asia-Europe logistics dependence corrected
eight to fifteen percent in January and February 2024. The European precision machinery
sub-sector traded at a P/E discount of one-and-a-half to two turns below its historical
average during Q1, with analysts explicitly citing supply chain risk in their
downgrades. Score: three out of five.

[SLIDE 19 — Scoring table and composite score]

With weights reflecting this company's business model — forty-five percent to
operational, twenty to structural, twenty to market, fifteen to regulatory — the
composite score comes to three-point-eight-five.

Elevated risk. Which would have triggered the deep geopolitical due diligence protocol
and evaluation of active hedges: analysis of the company's capacity to absorb higher
logistics costs without destroying margin, assessment of flexibility to diversify
suppliers outside the Asia-Suez corridor, and a defensive position in the equity or in
sector derivatives.

[SLIDE 20 — Case lesson]

I want to pause here and make a point that I think is central to how you should use
this framework.

Yemen's structural fragility — layer one — had been visible for nearly a decade. The
Houthis had been targeting Red Sea shipping since 2022, albeit less systematically.
The company's dependence on that corridor was information available in its annual
reports, in its P&L, in its supplier contracts.

Geopolitical analysis of an asset does not begin when the crisis erupts. It begins at
initial due diligence. The investors who had incorporated the Red Sea corridor's
vulnerability into their operational risk analysis were positioned to act when the
escalation came. Those who had not incorporated it were left reacting.

## [26:00 – 28:00] BLOCK 6 · Close and Call to Action

[SLIDE 21 — Synthesis: "Three ideas to take away"]

Let us close.

In these twenty-eight minutes we have built an argument across three levels, and I want
you to take all three away with clarity.

First: geopolitical risk already has a price in markets. Not incorporating it into your
analysis is not a neutral position. It is implicitly pricing it at zero. And in nearly
every case, that is wrong.

Second: the ZRC Four-Layer Framework — structural, regulatory, operational and market
— allows you to disaggregate what would otherwise be a vague, unmanageable category
into variables with distinct horizons, mechanisms and reversibility profiles. That
converts qualitative analysis into actionable quantitative inputs.

Third: the geopolitical score does not replace analytical judgement — it structures it.
Its value lies in forcing explicitness: compelling you to name the exposures, the
layers at risk and the transmission channels before you decide. What you name, you can
manage. What remains implicit, you cannot.
